Responsible AI Use in Studies

A guide for university students

AI tools can be useful learning aids when used correctly. Acceptable use means using AI to understand, not to replace your own thinking.

  • Get difficult concepts explained in simple terms
  • Ask for examples that illustrate theories
  • Get help debugging code you wrote yourself
  • Use AI as a conversation partner to test understanding
  • Get feedback on structure and argumentation
Acceptable
"Can you explain how recursion works in programming? Give me a simple example so I understand the concept."

Certain ways of using AI violate academic standards and can have serious consequences for you as a student.

  • Asking AI to write all or parts of an assignment for you
  • Submitting AI generated text as your own work
  • Asking for answers to exam questions
  • Using AI to circumvent learning objectives
  • Copying AI generated code without understanding it
Not acceptable
"Write a 2000 word essay about climate change for me."

If you have used AI as a tool in your work, you should be transparent about it. Many universities now require you to disclose AI use.

Examples of referencing:

Copyright/Company. (Year). Title of tool [LLM]. https://url

OpenAI. (2023). ChatGPT. [LLM]. https://chat.openai.com/

Always check your university's specific guidelines for how AI use should be documented.

AI models can generate information that sounds credible but is completely wrong. This is called hallucination.

Examples include:

  • Fabricated academic sources and references
  • Incorrect facts presented with confidence
  • Nonexistent functions in programming languages
  • Wrong dates, names, or statistics

Consequence: Submitting an assignment with incorrect sources or facts can affect your grade and academic credibility.

AI models are trained on large amounts of internet data, which can contain biases and prejudices.

This can result in:

  • Cultural or gender based stereotypes
  • Western centric perspectives
  • Outdated or one sided information
  • Lack of representation of minority perspectives

Be critical of AI generated content, especially on sensitive or controversial topics.

When using AI tools, you share data with third party companies. Be aware of what you type in.

Do not share:

  • Personal information (name, national ID, address)
  • Sensitive information about other people
  • Confidential research data
  • Passwords or security information

The information you share may be used to train future AI models.

Over reliance on AI can hinder your own intellectual development. Learning happens through trying, failing, and thinking for yourself.

If you always ask AI for the answer:

  • You do not develop your own problem solving skills
  • You lose deep understanding of the material
  • You become less prepared for exams
  • You struggle when AI is not available

Academic integrity is about honesty in academic work. It means that the work you submit is your own, and that you give credit to other sources you have used.

The core principles are:

  • Honesty: Do not lie about what you have done
  • Trust: Teachers trust that the work is yours
  • Fairness: All students should have equal conditions
  • Respect: Respect others' intellectual work
  • Responsibility: Take responsibility for your actions

Submitting AI generated text as your own work is considered a form of plagiarism at most universities.

This applies even if:

  • The text is not copied from another student
  • The AI text is "unique" and does not exist elsewhere
  • You have made small changes to the text

Many universities now have tools that can detect AI generated text. Consequences can range from a failing grade to suspension.

Not all AI use is black and white. Here are some grey area situations:

  • Language correction: Often accepted, but check with your teacher
  • Rephrasing: Problematic if it significantly changes the content
  • Idea generation: Can be fine, but the ideas must be developed yourself
  • Structure suggestions: Often accepted as a starting point

Rule of thumb: If you are unsure, ask your teacher before using AI.

As a student, you are responsible for:

  • Knowing your university's rules on AI use
  • Being transparent about how you have used AI
  • Ensuring the work you submit reflects your own understanding
  • Being able to explain and defend everything you have written

Ignorance of the rules is not a valid excuse. Take responsibility for your own learning.
